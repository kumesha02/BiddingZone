<p>The&nbsp;<strong>Arduino Uno</strong>&nbsp;is an&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Open-source" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-source">open-source</a>&nbsp;<a title="Single-board microcontroller" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single-board_microcontroller">microcontroller board</a>&nbsp;based on the&nbsp;<a title="Microchip Technology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microchip_Technology">Microchip</a>&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="ATmega328P" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ATmega328P">ATmega328P</a>&nbsp;<a title="Microcontroller" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microcontroller">microcontroller</a>&nbsp;(MCU) and developed by&nbsp;<a title="Arduino" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino">Arduino.cc</a>&nbsp;and initially released in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-What_is_Arduino?_3-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-What_is_Arduino?-3">[3]</a></sup>&nbsp;The board is equipped with sets of digital and analog&nbsp;<a title="Input/output" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input/output">input/output</a>&nbsp;(I/O) pins that may be interfaced to various&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Expansion board" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_board">expansion boards</a>&nbsp;(shields) and other circuits.<sup id="cite_ref-Makerspace_1-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-Makerspace-1">[1]</a></sup>&nbsp;The board has 14 digital I/O pins (six capable of&nbsp;<a title="Pulse-width modulation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse-width_modulation">PWM</a>&nbsp;output), 6 analog I/O pins, and is programmable with the&nbsp;<a title="Arduino" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino#Software">Arduino IDE</a>&nbsp;(Integrated Development Environment), via a type B&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="USB cable" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USB_cable">USB cable</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-princeton_4-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-princeton-4">[4]</a></sup>&nbsp;It can be powered by a USB cable or a barrel connector that accepts voltages between 7 and 20 volts, such as a rectangular&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="9-volt battery" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9-volt_battery">9-volt battery</a>. It has the same microcontroller as the&nbsp;<a title="Arduino Nano" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Nano">Arduino Nano</a>&nbsp;board, and the same headers as the Leonardo board.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>&nbsp;The hardware reference design is distributed under a&nbsp;<a title="Creative Commons" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creative_Commons">Creative Commons</a>&nbsp;Attribution Share-Alike 2.5 license and is available on the Arduino website. Layout and production files for some versions of the hardware are also available.</p>
<p>The word "<a class="extiw" title="wikt:uno" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uno">uno</a>" means "one" in&nbsp;<a title="Italian language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language">Italian</a>&nbsp;and was chosen to mark a major redesign of the Arduino hardware and software.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>&nbsp;The Uno board was the successor of the Duemilanove release and was the 9th version in a series of USB-based Arduino boards.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>&nbsp;Version 1.0 of the Arduino&nbsp;<a title="Integrated development environment" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_development_environment">IDE</a>&nbsp;for the Arduino Uno board has now evolved to newer releases.<sup id="cite_ref-princeton_4-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-princeton-4">[4]</a></sup>&nbsp;The ATmega328 on the board comes preprogrammed with a&nbsp;<a title="Bootloader" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bootloader">bootloader</a>&nbsp;that allows uploading new code to it without the use of an external hardware programmer.<sup id="cite_ref-What_is_Arduino?_3-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arduino_Uno#cite_note-What_is_Arduino?-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
