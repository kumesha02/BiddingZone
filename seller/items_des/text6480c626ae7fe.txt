<p>A&nbsp;<strong>transistor</strong>&nbsp;is a&nbsp;<a title="Semiconductor device" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor_device">semiconductor device</a>&nbsp;used to&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic amplifier" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_amplifier">amplify</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a title="Electronic switch" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_switch">switch</a>&nbsp;electrical signals and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical power" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_power">power</a>. It is one of the basic building blocks of modern&nbsp;<a title="Electronics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics">electronics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transistor#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>&nbsp;It is composed of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Semiconductor material" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor_material">semiconductor material</a>, usually with at least three&nbsp;<a title="Terminal (electronics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminal_(electronics)">terminals</a>&nbsp;for connection to an electronic circuit. A&nbsp;<a title="Voltage" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltage">voltage</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a title="Electric current" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current">current</a>&nbsp;applied to one pair of the transistor's terminals controls the current through another pair of terminals. Because the controlled (output) power can be higher than the controlling (input) power, a transistor can amplify a signal. Some transistors are packaged individually, but many more in miniature form are found embedded in&nbsp;<a title="Integrated circuit" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit">integrated circuits</a>.</p>
