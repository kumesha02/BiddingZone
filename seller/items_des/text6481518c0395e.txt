<p>A&nbsp;<strong>diode</strong>&nbsp;is a two-<a title="Terminal (electronics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminal_(electronics)">terminal</a>&nbsp;<a title="Electronic component" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_component">electronic component</a>&nbsp;that conducts&nbsp;<a title="Electric current" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current">current</a>&nbsp;primarily&nbsp;<a title="One-way traffic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-way_traffic">in one direction</a>&nbsp;(asymmetric&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical conductance" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductance">conductance</a>). It has low (ideally zero)&nbsp;<a title="Electrical resistance and conductance" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_resistance_and_conductance">resistance</a>&nbsp;in one direction, and high (ideally infinite) resistance in the other.</p>
<p>A semiconductor diode, the most commonly used type today, is a&nbsp;<a title="Crystallinity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallinity">crystalline</a>&nbsp;piece of&nbsp;<a title="Semiconductor" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor">semiconductor</a>&nbsp;material with a&nbsp;<a title="P&ndash;n junction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P%E2%80%93n_junction">p&ndash;n junction</a>&nbsp;connected to two electrical terminals.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diode#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>&nbsp;It has an&nbsp;<a title="Exponential function" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function">exponential</a>&nbsp;<a title="Current&ndash;voltage characteristic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current%E2%80%93voltage_characteristic">current&ndash;voltage characteristic</a>. Semiconductor diodes were the first&nbsp;<a title="Semiconductor device" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor_device">semiconductor electronic devices</a>. The discovery of asymmetric electrical conduction across the contact between a crystalline mineral and a metal was made by German physicist&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ferdinand Braun" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_Braun">Ferdinand Braun</a>&nbsp;in 1874. Today, most diodes are made of&nbsp;<a title="Silicon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon">silicon</a>, but other semiconducting materials such as&nbsp;<a title="Gallium arsenide" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallium_arsenide">gallium arsenide</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Germanium" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanium">germanium</a>&nbsp;are also used.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diode#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
