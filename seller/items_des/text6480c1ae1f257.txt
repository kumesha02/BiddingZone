<p>A&nbsp;<strong>capacitor</strong>&nbsp;is a device that stores&nbsp;<a title="Electrical energy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_energy">electrical energy</a>&nbsp;in an&nbsp;<a title="Electric field" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field">electric field</a>&nbsp;by virtue of accumulating&nbsp;<a title="Electric charge" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge">electric charges</a>&nbsp;on two close surfaces insulated from each other. It is a&nbsp;<a title="Passivity (engineering)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passivity_(engineering)">passive</a>&nbsp;<a title="Electronic component" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_component">electronic component</a>&nbsp;with two&nbsp;<a title="Terminal (electronics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminal_(electronics)">terminals</a>.</p>
<p>The effect of a capacitor is known as&nbsp;<a title="Capacitance" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitance">capacitance</a>. While some capacitance exists between any two electrical conductors in proximity in a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Electric circuit" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_circuit">circuit</a>, a capacitor is a component designed to add capacitance to a circuit. The capacitor was originally known as the&nbsp;<strong>condenser</strong>,<sup id="cite_ref-duff_1-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitor#cite_note-duff-1">[1]</a></sup>&nbsp;a term still encountered in a few compound names, such as the&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Condenser microphone" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condenser_microphone">condenser microphone</a></em>.</p>
